Evolution of massive galaxies and galaxy clusters.

Our Universe was most active 10-11 billion years ago. At this time, the bulk of the star formation was enshrouded in the most dust-obscured galaxies, which form stars at rates higher than 100 solar masses per year (DSFGs; see a review by Casey, Narayanan & Cooray, 2014). To date, many questions regarding the nature of these dusty galaxies remain unresolved. What are the fundemental drivers of these extreme star formation in the early Universe (violent galaxy interactions/mergers like their local counterparts or secularly evolving processes like smooth accretion of gas)? What are the relative contributions of these processes to the cosmic star formation history? How do these dusty galaxies link to the first collapses of the Universe's largest structure - massive galaxy clusters.


I have been tackling this issue through the combination of multiwavelength observational data and hydrodynamical simulations. My early work on characterizing more than 2000 DSFGs selected by Herschel observations show that galaxy interactions/mergers are responsible to trigger more than half of these galaxies at z~1 (Hung et al. 2013). This link between galaxy mergers and DSFGs has important implications on the assembly of massive galaxy clusters. Several progenitors of massive galaxy clusters at z~2-3 host overabundance of DSFGs with overdensities greater than 10 (see Hung et al. 2016b for an example of such protocluster, and Casey 2016 for a summary paper). A possible explaination for these DSFG-rich protoclusters is that the build-up of massive structure in the early Universe may be highly episodic rather than a steady process lasting several billion year. A few undergraduate researchers have been working with me on this topic through characterizing overall population of galaxy pairs and gas content in these large structures to better understand the nature of these DSFG-rich protoclusters.


      My recent work (Hung et al. 2016b) on a DSFG-rich proto galaxy cluster at z~2.1. This structure sits in the center of the COSMOS/CANDELS field. The rich ancillary data allow us to study the population of DSFGs, X-ray luminous AGNs, galaxy morphology, stellar masses, and SFRs in this structure.

Calibrating observational tools we use to study high-z galaxies.

Our observational tools to study high-z DSFGs can be limited and sometimes biased. For example, large-scale tidal features commonly seen in nearby galaxy mergers often fade away or become less prominent when artificially redshifted to high-z. I have compiled a set of nearby well-studied DSFGs (ultraluminous infrared galaxies) and simulate how their high-resolution imaging and integral field spectrosgraph (IFS) observations degrade at high-z. With this I show that when identifying merger signatures (e.g., tidal tails, bridges) visually, at least 20% of the high-z mergers can be missed simply due to data differences like surface brightness dimming and band-shifting (Hung et al., 2014). Contrary to expectations, interaction-induced disturbances in galaxy dynamics (2D velocity and velocity dispersion maps) are often absent in late-stage galaxy mergers with resolutions (~1 kpc) comparable to high-z observations (Hung et al., 2015). My follow-up analysis using hydrodynamic simulations confirm these conclusions with various initial conditions of galaxy interactions.
